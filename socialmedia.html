The goal was to demonstrate the most basic functionality: authentication and sending a plain post. Reflection: In class discussion and Pre class Readings

One discussion question that significantly shaped my thinking was: “Is a bot inherently deceptive, or does its morality depend on how it is designed and disclosed?” That question pushed me to move beyond the assumption that automation is automatically unethical. Instead, I began to see morality as dependent on transparency and intent. For example, a bot that clearly identifies itself and shares useful information (like stock prices) feels very different from a bot that pretends to be human to influence opinions. The design choice of disclosure became central to how I think about ethical responsibility.

Another discussion question that influenced me was: “Should bots have the same participation rights as human users?” This made me think about fairness and scale. When I programmatically liked someone's post using its URI, I realized how easily engagement can be automated. If scaled up, bots could artificially amplify content in ways humans cannot match. That raised concerns about whether automation distorts public discourse, even if actions such as a single “like” seems harmless.

The reading Do Social Media Bots Dream of Electric Sheep? helped me understand that bots are not one single type of actor. In the Introduction and Section 4, the authors categorize bots into different types, including benign informational bots and malicious influence bots. This categorization shaped my thinking because it framed morality as contextual rather than binary. A stock-price bot that shares accurate financial data serves an informational purpose, while a bot designed to spread misinformation operates very differently—even if both use similar technical structures. The reading made me realize that ethical evaluation depends on function and impact, not just on whether something is automated. Ultimately, building and reflecting on my own bot made morality feel less abstract. Ethical considerations are embedded in everyday design choices—what data is used, how posts are formatted, whether automation is disclosed, and how interaction is scaled.

Lingering Questions

    A lingering question is whether platforms should require mandatory bot labeling. Transparency seems ethically preferable, but enforcement may be difficult, especially with malicious actors. This creates tension between ethical ideals and what we can actually implement.
