Justification of 2-Bit Addition Circuit

My 2-bit addition circuit was designed using three half-adders connected with an OR gate to properly handle carry bit from previous bits. Each half-adder computes a sum and a carry for its respective input bits, and the OR gate ensures that any carry from prior additions is correctly included in the next stage. Tracing all input combinations confirmed that both the sum and final carry output are accurate. The same approach was applied to my 2-bit subtraction circuit, highlighting the symmetry between addition and subtraction logic.

Thoughts from Pre-Class Readings

The pre-class readings shaped my thinking by emphasizing that computers are not just machines but systems that process and transform information (inputs and outputs). The Computer Recreations reading (Metaphysics, n.d.) highlighted how even simple circuits, like our 2-bit adder, mirror logical reasoning, showing the link between physical computation and abstract problem-solving. The Integrating Living Organisms into Interfaces study (Plops et al., 2022) expanded this perspective, illustrating that computation can extend into dynamic environments, interacting with living systems and challenging traditional notions of what a computer can be. Together, these readings encouraged me to think about computers as both logical and adaptive systems.

Thoughts from In-Class Discussions

Our in-class discussions directly shaped my thinking by forcing me to question what qualifies as a computer. For example: “Is a broken computer still a computer?” and “Is a discharged/turned off computer still a computer?” made me consider whether a computer’s identity depends on function, state, or underlying structure. “Would computers/computation exist without humans?” challenged me to think about computation as a concept independent of human use, highlighting the difference between theoretical computation and practical application.

Lingering Questions

I still wonder how far computation can be extended before it no longer fits traditional definitions of a computer. Could systems that are autonomous, self-organizing, or integrated with biological processes challenge our current understanding of digital logic? How do we define a computer? Does everyone come up with their own definitions?
